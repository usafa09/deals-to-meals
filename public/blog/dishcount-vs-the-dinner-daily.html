Dishcount vs The Dinner Daily. An Honest Comparison from a Biased Source

By Bill McCormick · July 17, 2026 · 7 min read

Let's get the obvious out of the way first. I built Dishcount, so I'm not a neutral referee here. What I can be is honest, and the honest starting point is this. The Dinner Daily is a real service that's been doing deals-based meal planning far longer than I have, and if you're comparing the two of us, you're already smarter about grocery shopping than most people. Both products exist because of the same true idea. Dinner should start with what's on sale, not with what a recipe website thinks sounds good.

Here's how they actually differ, where they're each better, and how to decide.

The shared idea

Most meal planning apps work recipe-first. You pick meals, they build a list, and you pay whatever the store charges. The Dinner Daily and Dishcount both flip that. They look at your store's weekly sales first and build the dinner plan around the discounted items, so the expensive part of every meal is the part you get a discount on.

If you've read my other posts, you know I think this is the whole ballgame. The Dinner Daily has been proving the same point for years. On that premise, we agree completely.

What The Dinner Daily is

The Dinner Daily is a subscription service. You pick your store from more than 16,000 supported locations across all 50 states, set your family size and food preferences, and each week they send you a dinner menu built around your store's current sales. The recipes come from a curated library their team maintains, and the service has been refined over years of member feedback.

There's a free trial, and after that it's a small monthly subscription. Check their site for current pricing.

What they do well, and I mean this without a wink. Their recipes are tested and consistent, because their team curates them. The plans account for family size and common diets. The store coverage is wide. And there's a real company behind it with support if something goes wrong. If you want a done-for-you service with a track record, that's a legitimate thing to pay for.

What Dishcount is

Dishcount is a free website. You enter your zip code, see this week's actual deals from Kroger, ALDI, Walmart, and 80+ other chains, pick the sale items you want to cook with, tell it what you already have at home, and it generates dinner recipes around those exact items with real local prices, cost per serving, and a shopping list. If you shop a Kroger family store, you can push the ingredients to your Kroger cart in one tap.

There's no account required to browse, no subscription, and no trial clock. It's free because I built it for families like mine and I keep the operating costs low enough that free is sustainable.

What I'll admit without being asked. It's built and run by one person, me. The recipes are generated fresh from your deals rather than pulled from a tested library, which means they're tailored to exactly what's on sale near you, but no human cooked each one before you saw it. There may be misses, but you're not limited to only 5 recipes. You can generate additional recipes anytime you want.

The real differences

Price. The Dinner Daily is a paid subscription. Dishcount is free. Not free trial, not freemium. Free.

Where the recipes come from. The Dinner Daily matches your store's sales against a curated library of proven recipes. Dishcount generates recipes from the specific items on sale near you this week. Curation gives you consistency. Generation gives you plans built around your exact deals, including the random ones a fixed library might not cover.

How you use it. The Dinner Daily delivers a weekly menu to you. Dishcount is a live site you visit, browse, and build from. One is a service that hands you the plan. The other is a tool you drive. Some people want the first thing. Some people want the second.

Cross-store comparison. Dishcount shows deals from multiple chains side by side, so you can see whether Kroger or ALDI wins your week before you commit. A single-store menu can't do that.

The cart button. Kroger family shoppers can push Dishcount ingredients straight to their cart for pickup or delivery. If that's your store, it collapses the whole errand into a couple of taps.

Who should pick The Dinner Daily

Pick them if you want a hands-off service. If the idea of browsing deals and picking items sounds like a chore rather than a Sunday hobby, a curated menu that just shows up is worth a few dollars a month. Pick them too if tested-by-a-human recipes matter to you, or if your regular store is one Dishcount doesn't cover yet.

Who should pick Dishcount

Pick Dishcount if free matters, if you like seeing the deals yourself before committing to a plan, if you shop multiple chains and want them compared, or if you're a Kroger family shopper who wants the one-tap cart. Pick it too if you just want to try deals-first planning before deciding whether it's your thing, because trying it costs nothing and doesn't start a trial countdown.

The honest bottom line

You can test my claim for free forever and theirs for free for a couple weeks. See which plan your family actually eats, and which process you'd actually repeat when it's Wednesday and everyone's hungry.

I built Dishcount because I believe deals-first planning should be free and should show you the deals instead of hiding them behind a menu. The Dinner Daily believes a curated service is worth paying for. Both of us might be right, for different kitchens. Yours gets the deciding vote.
