Kroger Weekly Ad Meal Plan. How I Build 5 Dinners from the Ad Every Week

By Bill McCormick · July 17, 2026 · 7 min read

For years my meal planning worked backwards. I picked recipes first, made a list, and paid whatever Kroger charged that week. Then grocery prices climbed and I flipped the operation. I opened the weekly ad first and planned dinners around what was actually on sale.

It works. My family eats well and our grocery bill dropped by real money, not coupon-clipping pennies. The catch is that doing it by hand takes most of a Sunday evening. This post covers the method start to finish, a real week as an example, and the shortcut I eventually built because I got tired of the manual version.

Why the ad comes first

Every Kroger ad has a handful of items priced to get you in the door. Grocery stores call them loss leaders. Pork shoulder at $1.99 a pound. Whole chickens under a dollar a pound. A 12-count of eggs at a price that makes no sense. Kroger runs these on a rotating cycle, so the same deep discounts come back every several weeks.

Here's the thing most shoppers miss. Those loss leaders are almost always dinner anchors. Proteins, mostly. If you build your week's dinners around two or three of them, the most expensive part of every meal is suddenly the cheapest thing in your cart. That's the entire trick. Everything else will fall into place.

When the new ad drops

Kroger's new ad starts on Wednesday in most regions. The digital version goes live on Kroger's site and app that morning. A few regions flip on different days, so check your store once and you'll know your rhythm forever.

One more Kroger quirk worth knowing. Many of the best prices need a digital coupon clipped to your loyalty card before checkout. The shelf tag will say so in small print and will usually have a QR code you can scan to add the coupon in your Kroger app. If a deal in the ad seems too good, look for the words "with digital coupon" before you plan a week around it.

The method, step by step

Step 1. Scan the ad for proteins first. Ignore the snacks and sodas on page one. Go straight to the meat and seafood pages and find the two or three deepest discounts. Compare against the regular price when it's shown. A real deal is 30 percent off or better.

Step 2. Pick deals that stretch. A 6 pound pork shoulder is two dinners, pulled pork sandwiches one night and pork tacos the next. A family pack of chicken thighs splits into two meals. One deal that covers two dinners beats two deals that cover one each.

Step 3. Build each dinner around its deal. Now you pick recipes, but only ones whose main ingredient is already in your cart at the sale price. Fill in sides from whatever produce and pantry staples are also in the ad. Frozen vegetable bags and rice or pasta sides go on sale constantly and round out a plate for a few dollars.

Step 4. Check what you already have. Before anything goes on the list, walk the pantry and freezer. Half the supporting cast is usually already in the house. The list should include the sale items plus the true gaps, nothing else.

Step 5. Write the list from the plan, not the ad. The ad is full of good prices on things you don't need. If it's not part of one of the five dinners and it's not a true staple you'll use, it stays at the store. A deal on something you didn't need isn't savings. It's just spending with better marketing.

A real week, worked all the way through

Here's an actual week from the Kroger ad in my area, mid July 2026, so you can see the method produce real dinners at real prices.

The anchor deals that week. Pork shoulder at $1.99 a pound, down from $2.99. Cooked extra large shrimp at $6.00, normally $9.99. Eastern Seas scallops at $12.00, down from $17.99. Nature's Own honey wheat bread at $2.49, down from $4.29. Knorr rice sides at $1.00 a box.

Dinner 1 and 2. Slow-cooker pulled pork. A 6 pound bone-in butt ran about $12 and fed us twice. Chicken broth, paprika, onion powder, garlic powder, salt, pepper, 8 hours on low. Sandwiches on the sale bread night one, pork tacos night two.

Dinner 3. Bacon and butter shrimp over cheddar broccoli rice. The $6 shrimp, bacon, and two $1 rice boxes. About $13 in sale items for a dinner that feels like a restaurant made it.

Dinner 4. Pan-seared scallops. The kind of thing I never buy at full price, but $6 off is my splurge threshold. Pat them dry, sear in butter, 12 minutes start to finish.

Dinner 5. Chicken thigh skillet. Thighs were the fifth solid protein deal that week. Browned in a pan with mushrooms and onion, chicken broth and couscous added, cheese grated over the top.

Five dinners, and the proteins for all of them cost less than what two of those meals would run at regular prices. That's a normal week, not a jackpot week.

The mistakes I made so you can avoid them

Trusting the word "sale" without the math. Some ad items are barely marked down. If the regular price isn't shown, I look it up before planning around it. A real anchor deal is a real discount.

Buying deals without a dinner attached. Early on I'd grab every good price and end up with a freezer full of orphan ingredients. Now the rule is simple. No deal goes in the cart without a dinner assigned to it.

Missing the digital coupon fine print. I once planned a week around a price that needed a clip I never clipped. The register doesn't care about your meal plan. Check the fine print, clip before you shop.

Ignoring per-pound versus per-package pricing. Meat ads mix the two, and a great-looking package price can hide a mediocre per-pound number. Always convert to per pound before comparing.
